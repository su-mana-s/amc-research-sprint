# Intrusiveness as a function of uncertainty and risk
## The Logic of Arms Inspection

Arms control treaties are not designed to be thorough. They are designed to be proportionate. The regimes that demand the deepest access — on-site inspections, real-time monitoring, intrusive verification chains — cluster around a specific condition: high uncertainty about compliance and high cost if that uncertainty resolves badly.
<br>

The Alva Myrdal Centre for Nuclear Disarmament dataset bears this out. Across weapons control agreements, intrusiveness correlates with verification difficulty, not with the number of signatories or the formality of the institution. Nuclear regimes dominate the upper end of the access scale. They do so not because nuclear diplomacy is more sophisticated, but because nuclear capability is notoriously difficult to verify, and the consequences of verification failure are terminal.
<br>

We can use more than 80 years of data from nuclear safety treaties as a starting point for thinking about AI arms control. States historically have accepted intrusiveness when uncertainty of compliance is highest and risk of verification failure is intolerable. That logic similarly applies to AI. These learnings, however, do not transfer directly.
<br>

Nuclear arms control verifies tangible outputs: warheads, delivery systems, fissile material. Nuclear development and access is also constrained to government bodies, and deployment to high level officials. In contrast, AI control must verify behaviour, capabilities, and processes. Its development is in the hands of private corporations and non-profits, and its capabilities available to a myriad of users. The object of risk has shifted; therefore, the inspection architecture must shift with it.
